Museum Tales

An immersive AR storytelling journey layered over the museum experience.

Explore ↓

Motivation

Museums are often designed as spaces for observation rather than participation. Visitors typically move from one exhibit to another, reading labels and passively receiving information. However, this traditional model overlooks a core characteristic of museums: they are not only repositories of artifacts but also carriers of human stories, emotions, and cultural exchange.

Our project—"Museum Tales"—specifically focuses on the Xi’an Jiaotong-Liverpool University Museum, aiming to transform the museum experience from passive viewing to active storytelling. Inspired by the narrative structure of our exhibition script, we found that each artifact carries multiple perspectives: the university archivist, the Chinese language assistant, the campus reporter, and the museum volunteer often interpret the same exhibit in completely different ways. These diverse voices show that the meaning of a museum is not fixed but co-constructed by its visitors.

We chose to explore this theme through role-playing and an interactive system because it allows users to truly step into the museum narrative rather than just observe. By combining AR-based scanning interactions, identity-driven gameplay, and fragment-collection mechanics, users can engage with exhibits, converse with virtual characters, and ultimately curate their own micro-exhibition.

The Gap

The guiding philosophy of this project is that museums do more than display history—they invite every visitor to become a storyteller. Every interaction, every choice, allows users to find their own place within the cultural narratives of the museum.

While immersive technologies like AR can enrich visitor experiences, existing research and products reveal significant gaps that limit their full potential. Understanding these gaps helps us design a system that truly engages visitors in meaningful, interactive storytelling.

In recent years, both academia and industry have explored the application of augmented reality (AR) and immersive technologies in museums, yet several limitations remain. Damala et al. (2016) reviewed the state of AR in museums, highlighting its ability to enhance spatial learning, increase engagement through interactive overlays, and support multiple narratives. However, their study lacked evaluation of long-term knowledge retention, offered limited options for user personalization, and did not incorporate leaderboards or other gamification mechanisms. Vayanou et al. (2017) investigated story-based user engagement strategies in cultural heritage, finding that narrative-driven exploration effectively promotes visitor reflection and provides clear UX design guidelines. Nevertheless, their approach lacked real-time multiplayer interaction, offered limited support for diverse learning styles, and did not include cloud-based progress tracking. Bekele et al. (2018) combined AR with role-playing to propose an immersive learning framework, using branching scenarios to quantify user engagement. Yet their study had a small sample size, lacked leaderboards and competitive mechanisms, and provided limited support for repeat play. Sylaiou et al. (2010) assessed user experiences in virtual museums, analyzing narrative comprehension using both qualitative and quantitative metrics; however, NPC interaction was limited, cloud-based user profiles or leaderboards were absent, and device compatibility was constrained.

In commercial practice, Smartify App offers convenient exhibit scanning and multilingual information coverage, but lacks role-playing mechanics and story reconstruction functionality. Museum AR by Zappar provides engaging AR animations and intuitive scanning, yet does not support persistent user profiles, branching narratives, or leaderboards. TimeLooper VR Experiences delivers immersive historical reenactments with strong narrative focus, but is limited to VR, lacking identity selection and interactivity. Google Arts & Culture AR provides high-quality 3D displays and access to global collections, yet user agency is limited, gamification feedback is absent, and local museum content is insufficient.

Overall, existing research and products reveal clear gaps in enhancing user immersion, supporting multiple role identities, providing personalized and repeatable play, and integrating leaderboards or cloud-based progress tracking. These gaps highlight specific directions for improvement in the present project.
